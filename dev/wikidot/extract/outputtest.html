The Assessment Task

\n

The assessment task means re-evaluating every single student solution not just for right and wrong but for what it does or does not reveal about her skills. If all we have is right/wrong you can't map it onto the levels of the rubric without making silly assumptions. In many cases the artifact item simply does not contain enough detail to detect the elements that are built into the levels. At best you could say \"getting number 5 right counts as GOOD on outcome 3,\" deciding ex ante that the item does not give student chance to demonstrate excellence on that outcome.

\n

One could take a slap-dash-crank-something-out approach, but to do anything that had any validity I think we end up spending many more hours than the result would be worth.

\n

All told we have something like ~1800 items in the artifact collection (5 different ones). First step is to map the items onto the 6 goals - each item tagged with one or more goals it arguably measures/detects/provides opportunity for competence demonstration. We have about 50 separate questions/problems. Allow 2 hours for this task. It only need be done once.

\n

Next we evaluate each of these items for how getting it right/wrong or partially right/wrong would map onto the levels in the rubric. Assume each item matches just one learning goal. This means only 50x4 evaluations. Allow 1 minute of thought and note taking for each: 3.3 hours.

\n

Next obtain answer keys for exams and create Excel spreadsheet for recording levels for each item. Allow 1 hour for clerical set up.

\n

Review each artifact, recording learning outcome level for each question. Assuming just one goal per question/problem this means only about 1800 decisions. Assume each one can be completed and recorded in 30 seconds. Total time: 900 minutes = 15 hours.

\n

Transfer data from spreadsheet to TaskStream. Assume about 1 minute per artifact. Time: 1 hour.

\n

\n

The average hourly wage (very very crudely calculated - it's arguably much higher) for faculty is on the order of $50. Thus, the actual cost of the assessment is something like $4000 plus administrative costs). If the standard stipend offered to faculty is $500 that means (1) it's being remunerated at something like $15-20/hour, and (2) to get the task done faculty members effectively donate more to the task than the institution allocates for it.

\n

To actually assess the overall cost we need to include the effort of the faculty members who teach these courses and their respective department heads. Between this expense and that of the Office of Institutional Research a conservative estimate might be another 40 hours of work bringing the project total up to around $5-$6,000.

Learning Outcomes

\n

And what will we learn from this? Our motivation here is to determine whether our quantitative requirement as implemented is achieving its goals. Based on a sample of about 100 students taking courses that are tagged with this requirement (out of perhaps 1000 course takings?), using a metric that's not all that well designed to capture variations in the outcomes described, what will we get? A distribution of sample students on each of six outcomes. We CANNOT take the weighted average of these (that is, count poor as 1, fair as 2, etc.) since we have done nothing at all to ascertain whether we have an interval scale (we almost certainly do not).

\n
\n

So, how would we make changes to our quantitative requirements based on this? I do not think we could. The only reasonable outcome would be to revise our assessment methods.

\n

That's what I think we should do.

\n

NOTE that this will NOT code directly into the levels of the rubric. What it will yield is a score for each outcome: what fraction of opportunities to demonstrate this competence resulted in a demonstration of this competence.

\n

Long Term Solution

\n

Elaborate the goals a bit so that they have enough concreteness than any instructor could easily tag exam questions, problems, etc. with the outcomes the item \"detects\" and offer a key to get from the grade on the question or problem to the level in the rubric.

\n

The process of so formulating things should be iterated with an examination of how the data will be used. What policy and curriculum decisions do we imagine might be informed by this data? Based on the answer to that question, what should this data look like. To ignore this part of the process is to waste people's time and the institution's resources.

\n

THIS MUST be done in such a manner that it makes teaching the course LESS NOT MORE difficult. That's a challenge, but if assessment is going to be a sustainable part of what we are doing, then it must be so.

Course Schedule Policy Variables

\n\n\n\n\n\n
Efforts to reform, re-form, or just tweak the course schedule are frequently plagued with (perhaps innocent) self-serving motivations and then a mix of compromises and capitulations that leave a working majority of stakeholders at least willing to fold and get back to other things. Merit aid is rightly controversial but \"everybody does it.\" In its current form we rely almost entirely on its pecuniary qualities. Starting Points

We will assume a small college has a mix of full time tenured or tenure track (TTT) faculty and part-time non-tenure track (NTT) faculty and that the latter are paid at some rate that is lower than the former. In order for it to make sense to ask TTT faculty to \"do more\" we have to assume some combination of the following:

The Underutilized Professor

\n

Suppose we want to increase teaching loads because we think, on average, professors are not working hard enough. Let's do the numbers. Under our 75:15:10 profile, in a 40 hour week we would expect to see 30 hours of teaching. But if changing the load from 5 to 7 is OK because instructors are shirking, how much less than the expected 30 hours would the average TTT instructor have to be putting in per course for there to be room for another course each semester with no effect other than actually working full time?

\n
\n

Under a 7 course teaching load with an additional \"course\" for advising, an instructor would provide 4 measures of instruction each week (3.5 courses + 0.5 \"courses-worth-of\" advising). In 30 hours of teaching, this means averaging 7.5h for each course and 3.75h for advising. At this rate, the underutilized instructor teaching a 5 course load dedicates 2.5 x 7.5h + 3.75h = 22.5 per week to teaching.

\n

So, raising the teaching load from 5 to 7 courses per year is a no brainer if the average TTT faculty member is devoting only about 7.5 hours per week to each course she teaches.

Speeding Up the Line

\n

If instructors ARE already working 40 hours, maybe we can achieve a productivity increase by teaching faster. A simple analogy is speeding up the assembly line in a factory. In the time one used to do a certain amount of service, research, and teaching we will expect faculty to now do the same service and research but more teaching. What's the impact on students?

\n

If we teach 5 courses and advise as our teaching load now and this represents 75% of our appointment, then we spend 10 hours per course per week. Three of these hours are contact hours, 7 are for prep and evaluation (let's assume a 50:50 split of these).

\n

Over the course of the semester all students get 15 x 3 contact hours and the benefit of 15 x 3.5 prep hours = about 98 hours of \"instructional effort.\" If we assume enrollment of 20, each student also receives a slice of the evaluation time: 15 x 3.5 / 20 = about 2 1/2 hours of an instructor's time/attention.

\n

If we switch to 7 courses, instructors will speed up to 7.5 hours on each course. If 3 of these are still contact hours then the students get 15 x 3 + 15 x 2.25 = about 89 hours of instructional effort and 15 x 2.25 / 20 = 1.7 hours of evaluation and feedback.

\n

These represent a 10% drop in the time an instructor devotes to the overall class and a 35% drop in the amount of feedback, individual consultation, and evaluation students will receive.

Teachers Should Just Teach

\n

Maybe the right way to think about this is to shift instructional faculty away from a profile that includes research and service. Under current assumptions, what would this get us?

\n

If I am currently working 40 hours under a 75:15:10 profile and you switch me to a 100:0:0 profile and other things are held constant, what do we get? The ten hours I previously devoted to research and service are now available for teaching. Under the 5 course scenario I used 10 hours for each course and 5 for advising. This shift in profile will accomplish increased instructional efficiency AND make institutional governance more efficient by removing instructional faculty from that realm.

Dump Advising
\n

One possibility is to simply off-load some tasks. Advising, for example. So, let's have all academic advising done by student affairs professionals. Using our 40 hour, 5 course, 75:15:10 assumptions, this frees up 5 hours per week - over two semesters enough for an additional course.

\n

The savings represented by this move might be as much as 3/4 million dollars. If we assume 100 advisees per advising professional we could accomplish this by hiring maybe 10 student affairs staff and one manager at, say, 40k and 60k respectively, for total cost with benefits of under 550k.

\n

Increased incidence of \"what does the teacher want\" when advising is done by non-faculty.

\n

It is unclear how to reckon the reputation all and other pedagogical costs of decrease in faculty contact or academic content in advising. Further research needed on that.

We All Work Harder

\n

The simplest scenario is to assume that institutional sustainability can only be achieved by asking people to work more - we can be agnostic about how much or little people are working now - but if we can get some employees to work more hours then we can shed other employees.

\n

Assume we want the same types of classes, the same level of research, professional accomplishment, and service from faculty, but we want, say, 7 instead of 5 classes per year. Presumably the savings come from hiring fewer part-time instructors; a small institution might realize a savings of up to 1.5 million dollars, maybe 10-15% of instructional compensation cost, with this 30-40% increase in instructional work time.

\n

The non-instructional compensation cost is a slightly larger part of the budget so the savings might be even more on that side if the increase in instructional work can be accompanied by a similar increase in non-instructional staff work time. This could be implemented by having other employees add 12-16 hours to current working week. Employees might have the option to choose a work day that will shift from 9:00am-5:00pm to 8:00am - 7:00pm or to work 9 to 5 seven days a week. Assuming these extra hours would be able to cover the work of other employees, such a change would permit a small institution to move from, say, to cut up to a quarter of its work force saving perhaps 2 to 5 million annually.

\n

Note that on both the instructional and non-instructional side - maybe more on the latter - reduction in number of employees also increases efficiency by reducing managerial workload.

\n

If an institution has the wherewithal to handle the transaction costs, this plan could be individualized to take into account who is already working long hours, acknowledging that some folks are already performing a large amount of \"uncompensated\" work and exempt them from an increase but expecting the others to work up to the same level. In non-union factories this approach is sometimes used to reduce wages by setting piece rates according to the highest output workers and offering incentives to \"rate busters\" to keep the numbers high.

\n

These customizations will, of course, blunt the efficiency effect that motivates the change both by reducing the uptake and those transaction costs. But the bigger worry is that when you make people work that much more the usual result is a loss of quality which can be expected to reduce demand - in this case, enrollment.

\n

The bottom line is we need to be very rigorous about the costs of our cost-cutting.

Not a New Business Model

\n

One reason an institution might change the teaching load of full time faculty is to wean itself off adjunct faculty. Over-dependence on adjunct faculty has become a common business model in American higher education. It is a model that hides an unsustainable situation - unable to attract sufficient enrollment to its programs, an institution develops a menagerie of programs (often graduate and professional degrees) that can be staffed \"just hiring a few adjuncts while we get the program off the ground.\" If the instructional costs are low enough, any revenue is good revenue and so these programs proliferate and remain immune to close financial scrutiny.

\n

In those circumstances, one can say that adjunct labor IS the business model. When these places consider increasing teaching load so as to need fewer adjuncts they are not changing their business model at all. They are simply asking the full time faculty to do for free what the adjunct faculty were previously doing for a low wage.

\n

The resulting apparent savings do the real harm of obscuring even more the fundamentally unsustainable program mix the school is trying to recruit students to.

Assessment Should Track Deployment

\n

If an institution is going to deploy its instructional workforce on the basis of some T:R:S ratio, it should probably evaluate these individuals using the same proportions. The implications of this are simple: a promotion and tenure committee should give a person a score on research productivity, teaching, and service and these should then be weighted by the numbers in the TRS profile. A standard could be set: each to be evaluated relative to the rest of the college, for example, and some bar would be set that took into account the relative importance of the factors based on how we deploy people's time. But the traditional foregrounding of research productivity and disciplinary visibility is completely out of line in an institution with a high teaching load. It is organizationally stupid as it retains and rewards those who prioritize things other than what the institution does. It is ethically dubious because it rewards privilege and sends mixed messages.

\n

How does an institution pitch itself to prospective students, donors, and others? If that brand identity includes attention, personalization, and faculty interaction, the decrease in these outputs has to be figured in the savings. One trend in mass higher education is to reduce the \"personal\" on the instructional side and replace it with \"personal\" on the academic support and student services side.

Course Schedule Policy Variables

\n\n\n\n\n\n
Efforts to reform, re-form, or just tweak the course schedule are frequently plagued with (perhaps innocent) self-serving motivations and then a mix of compromises and capitulations that leave a working majority of stakeholders at least willing to fold and get back to other things. It is rare that the outcome of the process is measure against any goals that the changes were supposed to accomplish. It is a policy-making 101 blunder, but common. An antidote is to lay out a description of what is wrong with the status quo and what one would hope to accomplish with a better system. There will be tradeoffs to be made among the benefits, but each proposal can be assessed in terms of its effects on these policy outcome variables.
\n
\n

Enhance the Humans

\n\n\n\n\n\n
Institutions should always maximize the advantage they take of their size. In small institutions this often means individuals can use local and personal wisdom and discretion to deliver better outcomes. But we can extract even more value from this if we focus on providing tools and infrastructure that enhances what the individuals can do. And avoid tools that do the opposite. Lots of things that are good for students and/or teachers do not scale, but for any institution that basically will not ever be big, this can be at least partly ignored. And on the other hand, sometimes tools that are great for working at scale actually decrease the effectiveness of folks working in smaller environments. The trick is recognizing when these are in play.

Re-Visioning the Course Schedule

\n\n\n\n\n\n
Some institutions, long ago, took real pride in the look, feel, and readability of their course catalogs. When catalogs went online and functionally merged with the registration process many turned into ugly web renditions of the schedule of course day and meeting times. Gone was any attempt to use the listing of courses as an opportunity to convey the shape of the opportunities for intellectual development that the curriculum represents. But this need not be so.

What's Wrong with the status quo Course Schedule?

\n\n\n\n\n\n
The existing course schedule is the result of years of accretions of accommodations to the needs of individual programs and even instructors. Some of its pathologies lie in how it is administered and \"enforced\" (as in, it isn't), but it also suffers from the absence of deliberate design. One contributing factor is probably that no one in the institution actually \"sees\" it. The closest we have to an experience of its shape and form is the hassle students experience when looking for courses and the experience of the registrar when trying to assign classrooms. We will begin, therefor, by visualizing the status quo.
\n
\n

Why Not Choose First, Schedule Later?

\n\n\n\n\n\n
The conventional approach to scheduling courses is to ask faculty or department heads what will be offered and then to ask either faculty or department heads or deans or registrars when these will be taught and then to present this to students so they can carve out their own education subject to the constraints of the schedule. Their choices are constrained by conflicts in the schedule itself and by conflicts between the schedule and the rest of their lives. At some level we judge the level of interest in various courses based on how many people enroll. The problem is that we leave a lot of information on the table not knowing how much of the final choices students make is due to the constraints imposed by the schedule.
